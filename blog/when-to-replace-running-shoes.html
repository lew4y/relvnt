How Often Should You Replace Your Running Shoes?

Runners talk a lot about shoes, but not nearly enough about when to retire them. Keep a pair going too long and you're more likely to feel aches, pains, and potential overuse injuries. This guide explains how often you should replace your running shoes, what signs to look for, and how to stretch their life without compromising comfort.

Typical lifespan of running shoes

Most running shoes don't have an exact expiry date, but there is a useful mileage range to keep in mind.

  • For most runners, shoes last roughly 600–800 km (about 370–500 miles).
  • Lighter runners or those with efficient technique may get closer to the upper end.
  • Heavier runners or those with a more forceful stride may feel the cushioning fade earlier.

Think of that range as a guideline, not a strict rule. The real answer depends on how you run, the surface you use, and the specific model. If you're still looking for your first pair, see our picks for best running shoes for beginners.

Key signs your running shoes need replacing

Instead of counting kilometers alone, look for these practical signs.

1. Flattened or "dead" cushioning

  • The midsole feels hard or "slappy" instead of soft and springy.
  • Your legs feel more beaten up after runs even if volume and pace haven't changed.
  • You notice more muscle soreness or joint discomfort than usual.

When the foam loses its rebound, your body ends up absorbing more impact with every step.

2. Visible wear on the outsole

Flip the shoe over and check:

  • Heavy wear on the forefoot or heel with smooth, bald patches.
  • Uneven wear on one side, which can affect your gait.
  • Deep lugs on trail shoes worn down to stubs.

If the outsole is very worn but the cushioning still feels okay, it's still worth replacing - grip and stability matter too.

3. Upper stretched or torn

  • Noticeable stretching where your big toe presses up.
  • Tears along flex points or around the lace eyelets.
  • Heel cup padding worn down, causing slipping or blisters.

A tired upper means your foot moves more inside the shoe, which leads to friction and instability.

How your body tells you it's time

Your body often gives you an early warning before the shoe looks worn out:

  • New aches in knees, shins, or hips without a change in training load.
  • Plantar fascia or Achilles feeling more irritated after runs.
  • Feeling more impact through your joints, especially on harder surfaces.

If everything else in your training is similar but discomfort is growing, consider that your shoes might be the culprit. This is one of the common mistakes beginners should avoid - wearing shoes well past their useful life.

How to track mileage on your shoes

You don't need to obsess, but rough tracking helps enormously over time.

  • Use your running app - most apps let you assign a pair of shoes and track total mileage automatically.
  • Keep it simple - jot down starting mileage and date in a notes app or on the shoebox.
  • Rotate pairs separately - if you run in more than one shoe, track each independently.

Even running just a couple of times per week, it's surprisingly easy to hit 600 km over the course of a year.

Can you extend the life of your shoes?

You can't stop foam from aging, but you can avoid unnecessary damage:

  • Rotate pairs - alternating between two pairs lets the foam decompress between runs.
  • Use them only for running - avoid wearing your running shoes as all-day casual shoes.
  • Let them dry properly - avoid heaters; remove insoles and dry at room temperature.
  • Clean gently - knock off dried mud and use mild soap and water when needed.

These habits won't double the lifespan, but they can help you get closer to the higher end of the normal range.

When to replace shoes earlier than "recommended"

Sometimes you shouldn't wait for a specific kilometer number:

  • If you significantly change your running style or intensity - for example, starting speedwork or longer long runs.
  • If you're prone to injuries and want the reassurance of fresher cushioning.
  • If the shoe never felt quite right and you're starting to dread every run in them.

Your comfort and enjoyment matter more than squeezing out every last kilometer.

Conclusion

As a rough rule, most running shoes are ready for retirement somewhere between 600–800 km, but your own experience and the shoe's condition matter more than the exact number. Pay attention to comfort, visible wear, and how your body feels. When in doubt, it's safer to replace a bit early than to push a worn-out pair too far.
